# 🏠 Project 5 — Remote Work Setup & Productivity Guide

*How I stay organized, focused, and high-output in a fully remote support role.*

---

## About This Document

I've worked fully remote for 4.5 years — handling 30+ customer interactions daily, maintaining a zero-backlog queue, and sustaining a 96–100% QA score without in-person supervision or management nearby.

This guide documents how I've built and maintained the habits, tools, and environment that make that possible. It's not about working harder. It's about building a system that makes doing the job well feel natural.

## Daily Routine — How I Structure My Day

### Before First Contact
- [ ] Log into all systems and confirm everything is working
- [ ] Check for system outages or known incidents
- [ ] Review any handoff notes from previous shift
- [ ] Clear inbox of overnight emails — triage by priority
- [ ] Set status to Available

**Why:** Starting the day reactive (jumping straight into tickets) leads to missed context. 10 minutes of preparation prevents 30 minutes of catching up.

---

### During the Shift
- Work through the ticket queue in priority order: urgent first, then FIFO
- Document every interaction *during* the call, not after — memory fades after high-volume stretches
- Set a 45-minute working block → 5-minute micro-break → repeat
- Keep Teams/Slack visible in the background for escalation messages from Tier 2

**Why:** Batch documentation after 15 calls leads to errors and missing details. Documenting live keeps accuracy high.

---

### End of Shift
- [ ] Ensure no tickets are left open without a status note
- [ ] Write handoff notes for anything mid-resolution
- [ ] Send any follow-up emails scheduled for today
- [ ] Review today's QA metrics if available
- [ ] Log out of all systems and VPN

**Why:** An unresolved ticket with no notes is a frustrated customer the next day. Handoff notes keep the team running smoothly.

## How I Stay Focused Working From Home

### Dedicated workspace
I work from a fixed location — not the couch, not the kitchen table. A dedicated space signals to my brain that it's work time, and it keeps background noise and visual distractions out of calls.

### Communication discipline
I keep Teams/Slack notifications on but set to non-intrusive. I respond within 15 minutes during shift hours — fast enough to be reliable, slow enough not to let notifications break my focus mid-call.

### Managing energy, not just time
High-volume support work is mentally demanding. I take actual breaks — away from the screen — rather than scrolling through my phone during "breaks." Real rest between call stretches keeps my patience and communication quality consistent into hour 7 of a shift.

### Staying visible to the team
Remote work can create the illusion that you're absent unless you actively communicate. I send a brief message at start-of-shift, flag anything unusual in the queue, and confirm end-of-shift handoffs. This keeps managers informed without requiring micromanagement.

## How I Handle Technical Issues During a Shift

No remote setup is 100% reliable. Here's my protocol when something goes wrong:

| Issue | Immediate Action |
|-------|-----------------|
| Internet drops mid-call | Switch to mobile hotspot — pre-configured and always ready |
| VPN disconnects | Reconnect immediately using saved credentials; if fails, restart client |
| CRM goes down | Log interactions manually in a local doc; transfer to CRM when restored |
| Audio issues mid-call | "I'm experiencing a brief technical issue — can I call you right back?" Resolve, call back within 2 minutes |
| Computer freezes | Hard restart protocol — all systems bookmarked for fast re-login |

**The rule:** Never let a technical problem become the customer's problem. Have a backup for every failure point.

## Tools I Can Pick Up Quickly

I've learned every tool I currently use on the job. My approach to new software:

1. Explore the interface before reading documentation — click through and see what makes sense
2. Find the keyboard shortcuts early — they matter in high-volume environments
3. Identify the 20% of features that handle 80% of use cases — master those first
4. Ask a teammate who's been using it longer — most tools have non-obvious workflows that aren't in the manual
